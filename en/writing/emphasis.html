Bold

Use bold for emphasis, but use it sparingly: the more you use it, the less effective it is.

Use bold to emphasize the negative

Bolding the negative (in appropriate circumstances) is effective in making sure people don't miss the negative. If you choose to bold the negative, bold the full form of the contraction.

If you have a list of steps someone can or can't do, or items they do or don't need to include, consider using a bulleted list with checkmarks and X icons. For more information about how to create and use bulleted lists, refer to: Lists.

Example: Use bold to emphasize the negative

You do not need to do your first tax return before you can get these benefits and credit.

Applicants cannot complete Part B themselves.

Use bold to emphasize the key dates or times

You can bold a key date or time you don't want people to miss. If this is the case, bold only the key date or time, not the whole sentence.

Example: Use bold to emphasize key dates or times

Your SR&ED claim will generally be processed within 60 calendar days of the date we receive your completed claim and your income tax return for the year.

The CRA aims to review your application and mail you a notice of determination within 8 weeks of receiving it.

If the recipient died before the scheduled month in which the CRA issued either the GST/HST credit or the CAIP, payments will no longer be issued in that person's name or to that person's estate.

Underlining

Use underlining for links only.

Example: Use underlining for links

You may also view the reference number in My Account.

If you have a spouse or common-law partner, they also have to file a return.

Italics

People with dyslexia or other reading disorders may find it difficult to read italicized text.

Don't use italics:

  • For design or decorative purposes
  • To emphasize a word or phrase (use bold sparingly instead)
  • For long passages of text, such as quotations
  • In page titles

Follow The Canadian Style's rules for applying italics for the following:

  • French and foreign words (article 6.03)
  • Latin terms and abbreviations (article 6.04)
  • Unlinked titles of publications (including acts and legislation) and works of art (article 6.05)
  • Legal references (article 6.06)
  • Mathematical, statistical and scientific material (article 6.11)
